# Iran Escalation -- Credit Playbook

> Seeded from 7 prior Claude conversations. All claims below require verification against primary sources before use in trade sizing or risk decisions.

## 1. Core Framework: The Analytical Error to Avoid

The central insight across prior conversations is that the market's default pattern-matching -- treating Iran escalation as generic "geopolitical risk-off" -- is analytically wrong. The correct framing is a **stagflationary oil shock**, which produces fundamentally different credit dispersion than a standard risk-off event. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

In a generic risk-off, correlations spike and spreads widen uniformly. In a stagflationary oil shock, there are clear **winners and losers** at the sector and issuer level. The alpha is in the dispersion, not the direction. Buying protection on the index while missing the relative value across sectors is leaving money on the table. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

## 2. Transmission Channels

Five channels, ordered by speed and magnitude of credit impact:

### Channel 1: Oil Price

The most immediate and mechanically understood channel. Brent and WTI respond within minutes to headline risk. Credit spreads in energy-adjacent sectors follow with a lag measured in hours to days. The key variable is not the spot price but the **duration of the price shock** -- a spike that reverses in two weeks has minimal credit impact; a sustained move above $100/bbl for 3+ months changes the fundamental picture for multiple sectors. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

### Channel 2: Hormuz Physical Disruption

Distinct from oil price. Even if oil can be rerouted, physical disruption to ~20% of global oil transit and ~25% of global LNG transit creates logistical bottlenecks that hit specific issuers asymmetrically. Insurance rates for tankers, shipping schedules, and inventory drawdowns all matter here. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

### Channel 3: Qatar LNG Exposure

Often overlooked. Qatar is the world's largest LNG exporter and geographically adjacent to any Hormuz conflict. Disruption to Qatari LNG exports would hit European gas prices hard and cascade into gas-intensive industries: chemicals, fertilizers, gas utilities. This is a distinct credit channel from oil. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

### Channel 4: Direct Infrastructure Risk

Strikes on Gulf oil/gas infrastructure (Saudi, UAE, Qatari facilities) represent a tail scenario that would produce a step-change in energy prices rather than a drift. Credit impact would be immediate and severe across the entire energy-dependent complex. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

### Channel 5: Consumer Demand Shock

The slowest-moving channel but potentially the most damaging to credit fundamentals. Sustained high energy prices act as a regressive tax on consumers, compressing discretionary spending, pressuring margins for consumer-facing corporates, and eventually feeding into employment and default rates. This is the stagflation channel. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

See: [[concepts/energy-sub-industry-decomposition]]

## 3. Scenario Matrix

### Scenario A: Contained

- Diplomatic posturing, limited proxy activity, no direct state-on-state strikes
- Oil: $75-85 range
- Credit impact: minimal, CDX IG stays inside 60
- Positioning: no action required

### Scenario B: Gulf Infrastructure Strikes (Current as of 2026-04-04)

- Direct or proxy strikes on non-Iranian Gulf infrastructure; retaliatory cycle without full closure
- Oil: $90-110 range
- Credit impact: sector dispersion widens materially; losers gap out while winners tighten or hold
- Positioning: dispersion trades, single-name CDS on vulnerable issuers, underweight airlines/autos/chemicals in cash portfolios

### Scenario C: Full Hormuz Closure

- Sustained blockade or mining of the Strait; insurance markets freeze tanker transit
- Oil: $130+ (potentially $150+ if sustained)
- Credit impact: broad index widening, but dispersion remains the dominant feature; some energy names tighten even as index widens
- Positioning: index protection via CDX IG payers + single-name longs in energy; cash raises in vulnerable sectors

### Scenario D: Rapid De-escalation

- Ceasefire, diplomatic breakthrough, or regime change
- Oil: retraces to $70-75
- Credit impact: spread compression, unwind of dispersion trades
- Positioning: take profits on dispersion, rotate back to neutral

- **CVX** (Chevron) -- similar profile, slightly more Permian-weighted
- **COP** (ConocoPhillips) -- pure upstream, most levered to oil price

### Midstream
- **EPD** (Enterprise Products Partners) -- fee-based, volume-driven, benefits from increased throughput

## 5. Sector Dispersion: Losers

### Airlines
- **DAL** (Delta Air Lines) -- most exposed to jet fuel; hedging book matters
- **UAL** (United Airlines) -- similar profile, Pacific routes add geopolitical risk
- **LUV** (Southwest Airlines) -- domestic-only but fuel is largest cost input; weaker balance sheet than DAL/UAL

### Autos
- **F** (Ford) -- **BBB-/Baa3 rating cliff**; a downgrade to HY would force selling from IG-only mandates and produce a spread dislocation disproportionate to fundamental impairment. This is the highest-convexity single-name short in the auto space. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]
- **GM** (General Motors) -- similar consumer demand exposure but more rating headroom than Ford
- **FMCC** (Ford Motor Credit Company) -- **weakest captive finance arm**; funding costs are directly tied to parent rating, and auto repos are running at **2009 levels**, signaling consumer stress is already present before any oil shock amplification. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

> **Note:** Auto repos at 2009 levels is a pre-existing condition, not caused by Iran. The Iran oil shock would accelerate an already-deteriorating consumer auto credit cycle. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

## 6. Index Overlay: CDX IG Payer Rationale

The portfolio-level hedge is a **CDX IG payer position** (buying protection on the investment-grade index). Rationale:

- In Scenario B (current), CDX IG widens modestly but the payer position provides negative correlation to the long cash portfolio
- In Scenario C (Hormuz closure), CDX IG gaps wider and the payer position provides meaningful P&L offset to cash portfolio drawdown
- In Scenario D (de-escalation), the payer position loses carry but the cost is small relative to the portfolio protection in adverse scenarios
- The payer is preferable to single-name shorts alone because it captures systemic widening, not just idiosyncratic risk
- Roll into current series; avoid off-the-run liquidity traps

The payer position is a complement to, not a substitute for, the single-name dispersion trades in Sections 4 and 5. [from prior conversation -- no raw source available]

## 7. Key Monitoring Variables

Ordered by signal value:

1. **Brent crude front-month** -- most immediate price signal
2. **Brent term structure (contango/backwardation)** -- distinguishes transient spike from sustained supply disruption
3. **Tanker insurance rates (War Risk premiums)** -- leading indicator of physical Hormuz disruption
4. **Qatar LNG spot cargo pricing** -- early warning on Channel 3
5. **CDX IG on-the-run spread** -- market-implied credit risk
6. **Ford 5Y CDS** -- canary for the IG/HY cliff trade
7. **Auto loan 60+ day delinquency data** -- consumer credit stress, monthly frequency
8. **Crack spreads (3-2-1)** -- refining margin indicator
9. **US Strategic Petroleum Reserve releases** -- policy response signal
10. **Diplomatic calendar** -- UN sessions, bilateral meetings, back-channel reporting
